The comparison has an important limit: the city did not actually operate the extended routes. The estimate therefore cannot show how many people would have used them or how much each passenger trip would have cost.

## Promising attendance findings, but no proof of cause

The evaluation examined work attendance at six participating employers that supplied consistent payroll data. Together, those employers accounted for 903 eligible workers. Evaluators matched each participating employer with a nonparticipating employer in the same industry and district.

During the three months before the pilot, employees at participating sites missed 4.7 percent of their scheduled late shifts. Employees at the matched sites missed 4.5 percent. During the pilot, the respective rates were 2.9 percent and 3.9 percent.

After adjusting for employer size and month, evaluators estimated that the difference in the change was 1.2 percentage points. The 95 percent confidence interval ranged from 0.3 to 2.1 percentage points.

That result does not establish that the van service caused the improvement. Employers were not randomly assigned to participate. Two participating employers changed supervisors during the pilot, and one introduced an attendance bonus. The matched employers may also have differed in ways the analysis did not measure. In addition, payroll records showed whether a shift was missed, not why it was missed. Evaluators therefore described the attendance finding as promising but not conclusive.

A rider survey offered further context but could not measure behavioral change. It was sent to all 738 people who completed a trip, and 486 responded. Among respondents, 78 percent said the service made accepting late shifts easier. Fifty-four percent said they had previously paid for a taxi or app-based ride at least twice a month, while 31 percent said they had previously walked for more than 30 minutes after a late shift. Fourteen percent reported missing at least one shift in the prior three months because they lacked transportation.

Because the survey was conducted only after the pilot and relied on people’s memories, those responses cannot show how behavior changed. Riders with particularly positive or negative views may also have been more likely to respond.

Demographic information was optional. Among the 591 riders who provided it, 64 percent identified as women, 33 percent as men, and 3 percent as nonbinary or another gender. Forty-two percent reported household income below $35,000. Twenty-seven percent reported a disability, although the survey did not distinguish disabilities that affected transportation. Because demographic information was unavailable for all eligible workers, the report could not determine whether any group enrolled at a higher or lower rate than its share of the eligible population.

## Safety, environmental uncertainty, and the next policy choice

Three safety incidents were reported. One van was involved in a low-speed collision with no injuries. In another incident, a rider reported threatening language from a second rider, after which the operator separated their future bookings. In the third, a driver ended a trip because a rider refused to wear a seat belt. No serious injuries were reported.

The small number of trips and the reliance on reported incidents mean the pilot cannot establish whether shared vans are safer or less safe than other late-night transportation options.

The pilot also did not measure greenhouse-gas emissions. Vans traveled 61,440 vehicle kilometers, including 19,870 kilometers without passengers. Evaluators noted that combining riders might avoid some individual car trips. At the same time, empty van travel—and trips taken by people who otherwise would have used a bus—could add vehicle travel. The available data were insufficient to estimate the net effect.

The evaluation presented three policy options. Cedar Bay could end the pilot and rely on existing overnight routes; extend the shared-van model for 12 months with a $1.5 million budget cap; or fund later fixed-route buses while limiting vans to workplaces more than 800 meters from a late-night stop.

The report did not recommend establishing a permanent program. It recommended a 12-month extension with three changes: require employers to contribute 20 percent of trip costs, reserve two accessible vehicles throughout operating hours, and test fixed departure windows on Fridays.

In November 2025, the city council voted 6–3 to fund that recommended extension. It began on January 5, 2026. Its results are not included in the six-month evaluation.</pre></details></td>
